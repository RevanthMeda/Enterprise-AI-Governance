# What to build next

## 1. Activity Dashboard per User

This is the highest-leverage next step.

Why now:

* you already have users, roles, workflows, notifications, evidence, and audit events
* the missing layer is a **personal work surface**
* this turns the system from a governance database into an operational daily tool

What it should show:

* approvals awaiting my review
* systems I own
* controls assigned to me or my team
* overdue evidence requests
* unread notifications
* recently changed high-risk systems
* tasks due this week

Why this matters:

* improves daily engagement
* reduces workflow latency
* makes each role feel the product is “for them,” not just for admins

Best role-specific variants:

* **CRO/CISO:** risk posture, overdue high-risk items, approval bottlenecks
* **Compliance Lead:** control gaps, evidence missing, framework coverage
* **Reviewer:** assigned approvals, pending assessments
* **System Owner:** owned systems, evidence requests, upcoming renewals
* **Auditor:** recent changes, exported reports, immutable activity slices

---

## 2. Integration Connector — pick exactly one

This should be next after the personal dashboard.

My recommendation:

* **Jira** first if your likely buyers are product, engineering, platform, or AI governance teams embedded with technical orgs
* **ServiceNow** first if your likely buyers are enterprise risk, compliance, GRC, banking, insurance, or highly formal ITSM environments

If you are unsure, **Jira is the easier and faster first connector**.

What the first connector should do:

* create a ticket from an approval workflow
* sync ticket status back into the platform
* optionally create tickets for overdue controls/evidence gaps
* link ticket references on system detail and workflow pages

Why only one:

* one deep connector is more valuable than three shallow ones
* integrations become sales accelerators when they solve a real workflow, not just “connectivity”

---

## 3. Multi-tenant Support

This is the big architectural step.

Only put it before integrations if you are about to onboard multiple customers into one deployment.

Why now:

* your feature set is rich enough that multiple organizations could realistically use it
* adding tenancy later gets more painful once connectors, dashboards, notifications, and exports are everywhere

What to design:

* organization table / tenant boundary
* tenant-scoped users, systems, controls, workflows, evidence, notifications
* tenant-aware RBAC
* row-level authorization everywhere
* tenant-specific branding/settings later
* tenant-safe exports and storage paths

Critical rule:
Do this **properly**, not halfway. If you start multi-tenant, enforce tenant scoping in every query and service path.
